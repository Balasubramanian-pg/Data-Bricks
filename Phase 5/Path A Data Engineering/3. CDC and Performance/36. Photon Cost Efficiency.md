# 36. Photon Cost Efficiency

Canonical documentation for 36. Photon Cost Efficiency. This document defines the conceptual model, terminology, and standard usage patterns.

> [!NOTE]
> This documentation is implementation-agnostic and intended to serve as a stable reference.

## 1. Purpose and Problem Space
Describe why 36. Photon Cost Efficiency exists and the class of problems it addresses.
The primary purpose of Photon Cost Efficiency is to optimize the use of photons in various applications, such as lighting, imaging, and optical communication systems. The problem space revolves around minimizing the cost of photon generation, transmission, and detection while maintaining or improving the overall system performance. This involves balancing factors like energy consumption, system complexity, and photon utilization to achieve efficient and cost-effective solutions.

## 2. Conceptual Overview
Provide a high-level mental model of the topic.
Photon Cost Efficiency can be conceptualized as a multi-dimensional optimization problem, where the goal is to minimize the cost per photon while satisfying the requirements of the application. This involves understanding the relationships between photon generation, transmission, and detection, as well as the impact of various system parameters on photon cost. The conceptual model encompasses factors like photon source characteristics, optical system design, detector sensitivity, and signal processing algorithms.

## 3. Terminology and Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Photon Cost | The total cost associated with generating, transmitting, and detecting a photon, including energy consumption, system components, and maintenance. |
| Photon Efficiency | The ratio of the number of photons utilized by the system to the total number of photons generated. |
| Quantum Efficiency | The ratio of the number of electrons generated by a photon detector to the number of incident photons. |
| Photon Utilization | The percentage of photons that contribute to the desired outcome, such as image formation or signal transmission. |
| Cost-Effectiveness | The ratio of system performance to photon cost, representing the overall value of the system. |

## 4. Core Concepts
Explain the fundamental ideas that form the basis of this topic.
The core concepts of Photon Cost Efficiency include:
* Photon source optimization: selecting the most efficient photon source for the application, considering factors like wavelength, intensity, and spectral bandwidth.
* Optical system design: designing the optical system to minimize photon loss and maximize photon utilization, using techniques like beam shaping, focusing, and optical filtering.
* Detector optimization: selecting and optimizing photon detectors to achieve high quantum efficiency, sensitivity, and signal-to-noise ratio.
* Signal processing: implementing signal processing algorithms to enhance photon utilization, reduce noise, and improve system performance.

## 5. Standard Model
Describe the generally accepted or recommended model.
The standard model for Photon Cost Efficiency involves a hierarchical approach, consisting of:
1. Photon source selection and optimization
2. Optical system design and simulation
3. Detector selection and optimization
4. Signal processing and algorithm development
5. System integration and testing
This model provides a structured framework for evaluating and optimizing Photon Cost Efficiency in various applications.

## 6. Common Patterns
Document recurring, accepted patterns.
Common patterns in Photon Cost Efficiency include:
* Using LEDs or laser diodes as photon sources due to their high efficiency and directional emission.
* Implementing optical systems with anti-reflective coatings, beam splitters, and optical fibers to minimize photon loss.
* Selecting photon detectors with high quantum efficiency, such as photomultiplier tubes or avalanche photodiodes.
* Applying signal processing techniques like photon counting, pulse shaping, and noise reduction to enhance photon utilization.

## 7. Anti-Patterns
Describe common but discouraged practices.
Anti-patterns in Photon Cost Efficiency include:
* Using incandescent bulbs or other inefficient photon sources.
* Designing optical systems with high photon loss, such as those with multiple reflections or uncoated optics.
* Selecting photon detectors with low quantum efficiency or high noise levels.
* Neglecting signal processing or using inadequate algorithms, resulting in reduced photon utilization and system performance.
