# 27. Medallion Best Practices

Canonical documentation for 27. Medallion Best Practices. This document defines the conceptual model, terminology, and standard usage patterns.

> [!NOTE]
> This documentation is implementation-agnostic and intended to serve as a stable reference.

## 1. Purpose and Problem Space
Describe why 27. Medallion Best Practices exists and the class of problems it addresses.
The Medallion Best Practices are designed to provide a standardized framework for evaluating and implementing medallion programs, which are used to recognize and reward excellence in various fields. The primary problem addressed by these best practices is the lack of consistency and transparency in medallion programs, which can lead to confusion, inefficiency, and unfairness. By establishing clear guidelines and standards, the Medallion Best Practices aim to promote fairness, accountability, and excellence in medallion programs.

## 2. Conceptual Overview
Provide a high-level mental model of the topic.
The Medallion Best Practices are based on a conceptual model that emphasizes the importance of clear criteria, transparent processes, and consistent evaluation methods. The model recognizes that medallion programs serve as a way to acknowledge and reward outstanding achievements, and that they should be designed to promote excellence, fairness, and accountability. The model consists of several key components, including program design, criteria development, evaluation processes, and award procedures.

## 3. Terminology and Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Medallion Program | A program designed to recognize and reward excellence in a particular field or industry |
| Criteria | The standards or requirements used to evaluate candidates for a medallion award |
| Evaluation Process | The method used to assess candidates against the established criteria |
| Award Procedure | The process used to select and notify winners of a medallion award |
| Transparency | The degree to which the medallion program's processes and decisions are open and accessible to stakeholders |

## 4. Core Concepts
Explain the fundamental ideas that form the basis of this topic.
The Medallion Best Practices are based on several core concepts, including:
* **Clear criteria**: Establishing well-defined and relevant criteria for evaluating candidates
* **Transparent processes**: Ensuring that all aspects of the medallion program, including evaluation and award procedures, are open and accessible to stakeholders
* **Consistent evaluation**: Using consistent and unbiased evaluation methods to assess candidates against the established criteria
* **Accountability**: Ensuring that the medallion program is accountable to stakeholders, including candidates, winners, and the broader community

## 5. Standard Model
Describe the generally accepted or recommended model.
The standard model for medallion programs consists of the following components:
1. **Program design**: Establishing clear goals, objectives, and criteria for the medallion program
2. **Criteria development**: Developing well-defined and relevant criteria for evaluating candidates
3. **Evaluation process**: Establishing a transparent and consistent evaluation process
4. **Award procedure**: Developing a clear and fair award procedure
5. **Program administration**: Ensuring that the medallion program is administered in a fair, transparent, and accountable manner

## 6. Common Patterns
Document recurring, accepted patterns.
Common patterns in medallion programs include:
* **Annual awards**: Presenting awards on an annual basis to recognize outstanding achievements
* **Peer review**: Using peer review processes to evaluate candidates and select winners
* **Public recognition**: Recognizing winners publicly through ceremonies, press releases, or other means
* **Continuous improvement**: Continuously evaluating and improving the medallion program to ensure it remains relevant and effective

## 7. Anti-Patterns
Describe common but discouraged practices.
Anti-patterns in medallion programs include:
* **Lack of transparency**: Failing to provide clear information about the evaluation process, criteria, or award procedures
* **Bias in evaluation**: Allowing personal biases or conflicts of interest to influence the evaluation process
* **Inconsistent evaluation**: Failing to use consistent evaluation methods or criteria
* **Poor communication**: Failing to communicate clearly and effectively with stakeholders, including candidates and winners
