# Workspace Structure Deep Dive

Canonical documentation for Workspace Structure Deep Dive. This document defines concepts, terminology, and standard usage.

## Purpose

The Workspace Structure Deep Dive topic exists to provide a comprehensive understanding of the organization and layout of workspaces in various environments, including software development, project management, and collaborative workspaces. This topic addresses the problem space of inefficient workspace organization, which can lead to decreased productivity, increased errors, and reduced collaboration among team members. By understanding the concepts and principles of workspace structure, individuals and teams can create and maintain efficient, scalable, and adaptable workspaces that support their goals and objectives.

> [!NOTE]
> This documentation is intended to be implementation-agnostic and authoritative.

## Scope

The Workspace Structure Deep Dive topic includes the following concepts and ideas:

**In scope:**
* Workspace organization principles
* Workspace layout patterns
* Workspace management best practices

**Out of scope:**

## Definitions

The following terms are used throughout this documentation:

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Workspace | A designated area, physical or virtual, where work is performed, and resources are managed. |

## Core Concepts

The Workspace Structure Deep Dive topic is built around the following fundamental ideas:

### Workspace Organization
Workspace organization refers to the way in which components, tools, and resources are arranged and related within a workspace. This includes the creation of a hierarchical structure, the establishment of workflows, and the definition of roles and responsibilities.

### Workspace Layout
Workspace layout refers to the physical or virtual arrangement of components, tools, and resources within a workspace. This includes the design of the workspace, the selection of tools and equipment, and the allocation of resources.

## Standard Model

The standard model for workspace structure is based on the following principles:

1. **Modularity**: Workspaces should be organized into modular components, each with a specific function or purpose.
2. **Hierarchality**: Workspaces should have a clear hierarchical structure, with components and tools organized in a logical and consistent manner.
3. **Flexibility**: Workspaces should be adaptable and flexible, allowing for easy reconfiguration and adjustment as needs change.
4. **Scalability**: Workspaces should be designed to scale, accommodating growth and changes in the team or organization.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Deviations from the standard model should be explicitly documented and justified.

## Common Patterns

The following patterns are commonly associated with workspace structure:

* **Centralized Workspace**: A single, centralized workspace that serves as the primary location for work and collaboration.
* **Decentralized Workspace**: A distributed workspace, where team members work from separate locations, and collaboration occurs through virtual means.
* **Hybrid Workspace**: A combination of centralized and decentralized workspaces, where team members work from a central location, but also have the option to work remotely.

## Anti-Patterns

The following anti-patterns are common mistakes or discouraged practices in workspace structure:

> [!WARNING]
> These anti-patterns often lead to maintenance or scalability issues.

* **Overly Complex Workspace**: A workspace with too many components, tools, or resources, leading to confusion, inefficiency, and decreased productivity.
* **Inflexible Workspace**: A workspace that is inflexible or difficult to reconfigure, making it challenging to adapt to changing needs or circumstances.
* **Under-Resourced Workspace**: A workspace with inadequate resources, leading to decreased productivity, increased errors, and reduced collaboration.

## Edge Cases

The following edge cases are unusual, ambiguous, or boundary scenarios related to workspace structure:

> [!CAUTION]
> Edge cases are frequently overlooked and may cause incorrect assumptions.

* **Remote Work**: Team members working from remote locations, requiring special consideration for communication, collaboration, and workspace management.
* **Multi-Team Workspaces**: Workspaces shared by multiple teams, requiring careful management of resources, communication, and collaboration.
* **Regulatory Compliance**: Workspaces subject to regulatory requirements, such as data security, privacy, or accessibility standards.
