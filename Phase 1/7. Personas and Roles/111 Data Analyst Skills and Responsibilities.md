# Data Analyst Skills and Responsibilities

Canonical documentation for Data Analyst Skills and Responsibilities. This document defines concepts, terminology, and standard usage.

## Purpose

The role of a Data Analyst is crucial in today's data-driven world, where organizations rely on insights and trends to make informed decisions. This topic exists to provide a comprehensive overview of the skills and responsibilities required to excel in this position, addressing the problem space of effective data analysis and interpretation. The goal is to establish a common understanding of the expectations and requirements for Data Analysts, ensuring they can effectively collect, analyze, and present data to drive business outcomes. This documentation is intended to be implementation-agnostic and authoritative, serving as a foundation for Data Analysts, organizations, and stakeholders to align their understanding and practices.

## Scope

**In scope:**
* Data analysis techniques and methodologies
* Data visualization and presentation best practices
* Data quality and integrity principles
* Collaboration and communication strategies with stakeholders

**Out of scope:**
* Tool-specific implementations (e.g., Excel, Tableau, Power BI)
* Vendor-specific behavior (e.g., software limitations, proprietary features)
* Advanced data science or machine learning topics
* Industry-specific regulations or compliance requirements

## Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Data Analysis | The process of examining data sets to conclude about the information they contain, often using statistical and analytical techniques. |
| Data Visualization | The presentation of data in a graphical or pictorial format to facilitate understanding and insight. |
| Data Quality | The degree to which data is accurate, complete, consistent, and reliable, meeting the requirements of its intended use. |
| Stakeholder | An individual or group with an interest or concern in the data analysis process or its outcomes. |

> [!TIP]
> Definitions should be stable over time; avoid contextual language.

## Core Concepts

### Data Analysis Life Cycle
The data analysis life cycle encompasses the stages of problem definition, data collection, data cleaning, data analysis, and results interpretation. Understanding this cycle is essential for Data Analysts to ensure that their work is thorough, accurate, and relevant to the business needs.

### Data Storytelling
Data storytelling is the ability to effectively communicate insights and findings from data analysis to both technical and non-technical stakeholders. It involves creating a narrative around the data, using visualization and presentation techniques to make the information engaging and actionable.

## Standard Model

The standard model for Data Analyst Skills and Responsibilities includes:
1. **Data Collection**: Gathering relevant data from various sources.
2. **Data Cleaning and Preprocessing**: Ensuring data quality and preparing it for analysis.
3. **Data Analysis**: Applying statistical and analytical techniques to extract insights.
4. **Data Visualization**: Presenting findings in a clear and compelling manner.
5. **Insight Generation**: Drawing meaningful conclusions from the analysis.
6. **Communication**: Effectively presenting results to stakeholders.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Deviations from the standard model should be explicitly documented and justified, as they may impact the validity or reliability of the analysis.

## Common Patterns

* **Agile Data Analysis**: Iterative and flexible approach to data analysis, emphasizing rapid insights and continuous improvement.
* **Collaborative Data Analysis**: Working closely with stakeholders to ensure that analysis meets their needs and expectations.
* **Data-Driven Decision Making**: Using data analysis to inform and support business decisions, rather than relying on intuition or anecdotal evidence.

## Anti-Patterns

> [!WARNING]
> These anti-patterns often lead to maintenance or scalability issues, and can undermine the credibility of the Data Analyst and the organization.

* **Lack of Documentation**: Failing to document data sources, analysis methods, and assumptions, making it difficult to reproduce or understand the analysis.
* **Insufficient Data Quality Checks**: Neglecting to verify data accuracy, completeness, and consistency, which can lead to flawed analysis and incorrect conclusions.
* **Inadequate Communication**: Failing to present findings clearly and effectively to stakeholders, resulting in misunderstandings or lack of action.

## Edge Cases

> [!CAUTION]
> Edge cases are frequently overlooked and may cause incorrect assumptions.

* **Dealing with Missing Data**: Handling situations where data is incomplete or missing, which can affect analysis outcomes and reliability.
* **Addressing Data Bias**: Recognizing and mitigating biases in data collection or analysis, which can lead to skewed insights and unfair outcomes.
* **Working with Large Datasets**: Managing and analyzing large volumes of data, which can pose challenges in terms of processing power, storage, and interpretation.

## Related Topics

* **Data Science**: The broader field of study that encompasses data analysis, machine learning, and related disciplines.
* **Business Intelligence**: The processes and technologies used to transform data into actionable insights that inform business decisions.
* **Data Governance**: The policies, procedures, and standards for managing data across an organization, ensuring its quality, security, and compliance.
